Privacy Policy

Effective 12 April 2026 · CorpseClient

1. Overview

This policy explains how the CorpseClient ecosystem (website, launcher, and related services) may process information. We aim to be transparent: the latest version always lives on this page.

2. Data we may collect

Depending on enabled features, this may include:

  • Account data: username, email, password hash (we never store plaintext passwords).
  • Technical data: launcher version, OS, UI language — for stability and diagnostics.
  • Network metadata: IP address and request time when calling our APIs — for abuse prevention and rate limits.
  • Local preferences: stored on your device (e.g. selected client version) and not uploaded unless needed.

3. Purposes

We use data to operate the service, authenticate users, provide support, analyse crashes in aggregated form, and meet legal obligations.

4. Cookies & local storage

The site may use a minimal set of cookies for theme and language. You can clear them in the browser — some conveniences may reset.

5. Third parties

We do not sell personal data. Sub-processors (hosting, CDN) receive only what is required to run the service. We may disclose information if required by law.

6. Retention

Account data is kept while you use the service. Security logs may be retained for a limited period (e.g. up to 90 days) unless a longer retention is needed for an investigation.

7. Your rights

Depending on your region, you may request access, correction, deletion of your account (where lawful), and opt-out of marketing if we ever add it.

8. Children

The service is not directed at children under 13 without parental consent. Contact us if you believe we collected a child’s data by mistake.

9. Changes

We may update this policy; the date at the top will change. Material updates may also be announced on the site or in the launcher.

10. Contact

For privacy questions, add a dedicated email or ticket URL when ready. Until then, use the Contact section on the home page.

Tip. Before launch, add your legal entity name, jurisdiction, and a data protection contact — many frameworks (including GDPR-style rules) expect this to be explicit.
